Abstract
Stationarity transformations are standard preprocessing in time series forecasting, yet their actual impact on accuracy across different non-stationarity types and model families has received little controlled evaluation. We construct synthetic datasets with known properties—trend, seasonality, heteroscedasticity, and combinations—and apply fourteen transformation configurations across seven models and three forecast horizons (3,528 experiments). Stationarity is quantified via consensus ratios from ten statistical tests, and each transform–dataset pair is classified as matched or mismatched based on whether the transform targets the dataset’s known non-stationarity. For matched pairs, transforms improve forecasts only 18% of the time. The primary exception is variance stabilization: log and Box-Cox on heteroscedastic data improve accuracy in 60–65% of cases. Differencing a linear-trend series—a textbook use case—worsens forecasts in all cases tested. Mediation analysis confirms that while transforms achieve trend stationarity, this does not translate into lower forecast error; the mechanism is signal attenuation. Real-world validation on TSA airport passenger data corroborates these findings. Our results suggest transformation selection should be guided by empirical out-of-sample evaluation rather than theoretical stationarity assumptions.

Introduction
Stationarity transformations occupy a central role in time series forecasting methodology. Textbooks and practitioner guides routinely recommend preprocessing steps such as differencing, log transforms, and seasonal adjustments to render a series stationary before model fitting
(
ref-BoxJenkins1970,
;
ref-Hyndman2018,
)
. The rationale is straightforward: many classical forecasting models—autoregressive (AR), autoregressive moving average (ARMA), and their extensions—are formulated under the assumption of stationary input, and violating this assumption may degrade forecast quality.
Despite the ubiquity of this advice, surprisingly little work has evaluated whether achieving stationarity through preprocessing
actually
improves forecast accuracy in a controlled setting. Most empirical forecasting studies either apply a fixed preprocessing pipeline without comparing alternatives
(
ref-Makridakis2018,
;
ref-Makridakis2022,
)
, or evaluate models on real-world data where the true data-generating process (DGP) is unknown, making it impossible to determine whether a transformation targets the correct form of non-stationarity.
This gap matters for practice. If transformations that successfully achieve stationarity do not reliably improve forecasts—or worse, degrade them—then practitioners would benefit from empirical guidance on
when
and
which
transformations help, rather than relying solely on stationarity as a preprocessing criterion.
This gap is addressed through a controlled experimental design with three contributions:
1.
Synthetic time series with known DGPs are constructed to isolate specific non-stationarity patterns—trend, seasonality, heteroscedasticity, and their combinations—enabling precise attribution of transformation effects.
2.
Fourteen transformation configurations are evaluated across seven forecasting models spanning statistical and machine learning families, and three forecast horizons, yielding 3,528 experimental configurations.
3.
A mediation analysis framework is introduced that decomposes the transformation–accuracy relationship into three causal pathways: whether transformations achieve stationarity, whether achieved stationarity improves forecasts, and whether transformations directly affect forecast error.
The results reveal that the relationship between stationarity and forecast accuracy is more nuanced than the conventional preprocessing pipeline assumes. Transformations do increase stationarity as measured by a weighted consensus of ten statistical tests. However, higher stationarity ratios are associated with
worse
forecast accuracy, not better. The total effect of applying any transformation is a statistically significant increase in sMAPE. This pattern holds even for AR and ARMA models—traditionally classified as requiring stationary input—though their implementations include trend parameterization (Table
2
) that partially accommodates non-stationarity internally, which itself underscores the diminishing practical necessity of external stationarity transforms. The primary exception is variance stabilization: log and Box-Cox transforms on heteroscedastic data improve accuracy for most model families by addressing heteroscedasticity without removing learnable signal structure.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 describes the experimental design. Section 4 presents the main results. Section 5 develops the mediation analysis. Section 6 provides real-world validation. Section 7 discusses implications, and Section 8 concludes.

Related Work
The assumption that stationarity is a prerequisite for accurate forecasting has deep roots in the time series literature. Box and Jenkins
(
ref-BoxJenkins1970,
)
established the ARIMA modeling paradigm, in which differencing is applied to achieve stationarity before fitting autoregressive and moving average components. This framework has been enormously influential, and its preprocessing logic—difference until stationary, then model the residual—remains standard practice
(
ref-Hyndman2018,
)
.
Subsequent work has expanded the transformation toolkit. Box and Cox
(
ref-BoxCox1964,
)
introduced the power transformation family for variance stabilization. Geweke and Porter-Hudak
(
ref-GewekePorterHudak1983,
)
proposed the log-periodogram regression for estimating fractional differencing parameters, enabling transforms that remove long-range dependence while preserving more information than integer differencing. Seasonal differencing addresses periodic patterns, and pipeline combinations (e.g., log followed by differencing) are common in practice.
Despite this rich methodological literature, controlled empirical evaluations of transformation effectiveness are scarce. The M-competition series
(
ref-Makridakis2000,
;
ref-Makridakis2018,
;
ref-Makridakis2022,
)
have advanced forecasting methodology enormously, but their focus is on model comparison rather than preprocessing evaluation. Participants typically choose their own preprocessing pipelines, making it difficult to isolate transformation effects.
Several studies have examined specific transformations. Nelson and Granger
(
ref-Nelson1979,
)
investigated the effects of Box-Cox transformations on forecast accuracy, finding mixed results depending on the data characteristics. Franses
(
ref-Franses1998,
)
examined seasonal adjustment and its impact on forecasting, noting that inappropriate seasonal adjustment can harm accuracy. More recently, de Prado
(
ref-Prado2018,
)
studied fractional differencing in the context of financial time series, finding that it can preserve useful long-range information.
However, no prior study has systematically evaluated a comprehensive set of transformations across multiple non-stationarity types, model families, and forecast horizons in a controlled setting where the true DGP is known. This work fills this gap by combining controlled synthetic experiments with a mediation analysis framework that disentangles the mechanisms through which transformations affect forecast accuracy.

3.3
Forecasting Models
Seven models are selected spanning three categories based on their theoretical stationarity requirements:
•
Traditionally stationarity-assuming:
AR, ARMA—classical formulations assume stationary input, though modern implementations (e.g., statsmodels) include deterministic trend parameters that partially accommodate non-stationarity internally.
•
Partial stationarity:
ARIMA—includes internal differencing but still benefits from stationary residuals.
•
Stationarity not required:
ETS, Holt-Winters, Prophet
(
ref-TaylorLetham2018,
)
, Gradient Boosting (LightGBM
(
ref-Ke2017,
)

This categorization enables us to test whether transformations help more for models that traditionally assume stationarity. We note that the distinction between “requiring” and “not requiring” stationarity is blurred in practice: AR and ARMA implementations with trend terms effectively estimate deterministic trend components alongside autoregressive structure, reducing the practical gap between these categories.

3.6
Hyperparameter Tuning
To ensure each model operates at its best configuration for each dataset–transformation combination, automated hyperparameter tuning is performed via randomized search. This is critical for fair comparison: a poorly tuned model might appear to benefit from (or be harmed by) a transformation when the real issue is suboptimal hyper-parameters.
Statistical models (AR, ARMA, ARIMA, ETS, Holt-Winters):
For each model, a parameter grid is defined over model-specific hyper-parameters and candidates are evaluated using in-sample RMSE. The search evaluates up to 50 random parameter combinations and selects the configuration with the lowest RMSE on the training data.
Machine learning models (Gradient Boosting):
Scikit-learn’s RandomizedSearchCV is used with 5-fold cross-validation and negative mean squared error as the scoring metric. The search evaluates 15 random combinations across 5 folds.
Prophet:
Uses a randomized search (50 iterations) over changepoint and seasonality prior scales, with an 80/20 train/validation split for evaluation.
All hyperparameter search results are logged for reproducibility and provided in the supplementary materials.

Results
The results tell a consistent story: stationarity transformations often hurt forecast accuracy in the controlled settings tested, with variance stabilization being the primary exception. Throughout this section, stationarity is measured via the filtered consensus ratios described in Section 3.7, which account for confounding between stationarity dimensions (e.g., excluding ARCH results when trend is present) rather than relying on any single test. The analysis begins by examining how transformations affect accuracy across different dataset types and model groups.

For models traditionally assumed to require stationarity (AR, ARMA), the none-transformation is the best performer for four of twelve datasets (linear trend, multiplicative variance, combined trend+seasonal, all components). 7
Discussion
These findings reveal a gap between the theoretical rationale for stationarity preprocessing and its empirical effect on forecast accuracy. In the controlled experiments presented here, the explanation is straightforward: many transformations, particularly differencing, remove not only non-stationarity but also the predictable structure that forecasting models exploit.
Consider a series with a linear trend. Differencing effectively eliminates the deterministic trend to satisfy stationarity criteria, but this comes at a steep cost: it acts as a high-pass filter that removes low-frequency signal components, which are often the most predictable for out-of-sample extrapolation. The resulting differenced series is closer to white noise, which is by definition unpredictable. This phenomenon is formally known as over-differencing
(
ref-PlosserSchwert1978,
)
, where unnecessary differencing induces a non-invertible moving average component that can attenuate forecastable signal. The issue is not that differencing is a “bad method”—it is a well-understood linear operator—but rather that the information trade-off between achieving stationarity and preserving predictable structure is often unfavorable in practical forecasting settings.
Perhaps more surprisingly, this harmful effect persists even for stochastic trends (random walks), where differencing is theoretically optimal. In the experiments, first differencing on random walk data produced sMAPE of 1.53—substantially worse than the untransformed baseline (0.31). This result likely reflects two factors. First, modern model implementations accommodate stochastic drift through internal trend parameterization (as shown in Table
2
), reducing the practical benefit of explicit external differencing. Second, on finite samples, the inverse transformation required to map differenced forecasts back to the original scale compounds errors: each forecasted increment is added cumulatively, so small per-step errors accumulate into large absolute deviations at longer horizons. Together, these factors mean that even in the canonical case for differencing, the operational trade-off becomes unfavorable in our experimental setup.
The variance stabilization exception is instructive. Log and Box-Cox transforms on heteroscedastic data do not remove signal—they reshape the error distribution to be more homogeneous, which genuinely helps models that assume constant variance. This suggests that the relevant question is not “is the series stationary?” but rather “does the transformation address a specific modeling assumption violation without removing predictive information?”
These findings should not be interpreted as invalidating the theoretical foundations of differencing in classical time-series analysis. Stationarity remains a fundamental theoretical property for understanding data-generating processes, ensuring model stability, and avoiding spurious correlations in inferential statistics
(
ref-BoxJenkins1970,
)
. However, this study focuses strictly on the operational trade-off between statistical purity and empirical out-of-sample predictive accuracy. The mathematical properties of unit root processes and the role of differencing in achieving stationarity remain well-established. Rather, the results suggest that in practical forecasting pipelines—involving modern model implementations, finite samples, hyperparameter tuning, and out-of-sample evaluation—achieving stationarity through preprocessing does not necessarily translate into improved forecast accuracy. Stationarity is a statistical property, whereas forecastability is an empirical predictive property; the two are related but not equivalent.
7.1
Implications for Practice
These results do not argue against preprocessing altogether; rather, they argue for selective, data-driven preprocessing that distinguishes between transformations that preserve predictive signal (such as variance stabilization) and those that inadvertently suppress it (such as over-differencing).
1.
Transformation selection should be guided by empirical evaluation on held-out data rather than theoretical stationarity assumptions
(
ref-Bergmeir2018,
)
. In practice, this could take the form of a validation-based selector: apply each candidate transformation, forecast over the most recent 2–3 held-out windows, and select the transformation (or no transformation) that minimizes out-of-sample error before deploying the final model.
2.
Variance-stabilizing transforms (log, Box-Cox) should be applied when heteroscedasticity is present—these address a genuine modeling assumption violation without attenuating predictive signal.
3.
Differencing-based transforms (first, seasonal, or fractional) should be applied with caution rather than as a default preprocessing step, as they act as high-pass filters that risk removing learnable low-frequency signal even when they successfully achieve stationarity.
4.
Pipeline combinations of transforms should be avoided unless empirically validated, as stacking transforms compounds information loss.
7.2
Limitations
Our study has several limitations. First, while our synthetic DGPs cover both deterministic and stochastic trends, they remain simpler than many real-world time series which may exhibit regime changes, structural breaks, and complex nonlinear dynamics. Second, our model set does not include deep learning approaches that may interact differently with preprocessing. Third, our real-world validation uses a single transportation series; broader validation across domains would strengthen the conclusions. Fourth, the stationarity consensus approach relies on asymptotic test properties that may not hold perfectly with our sample size of 200 observations. Fifth, for the autoregressive datasets, the AR(2) autocorrelation structure decays rapidly and provides limited predictability beyond a few steps ahead; at our minimum forecast horizon of
h
=
4
h=4
, much of the exploitable AR signal has already decayed, making it difficult to observe scenarios where differencing genuinely helps by revealing autocorrelated residuals. The conclusions should therefore be interpreted as evidence from controlled forecasting settings rather than universal claims about all nonstationary processes.
8
Conclusions
This study provides controlled experimental evidence that stationarity transformations often fail to improve time series forecast accuracy. Through a mediation analysis of 3,528 experimental configurations, it is shown that while transformations successfully achieve stationarity, this achieved stationarity is associated with worse—not better—forecast accuracy. The mechanism is signal attenuation: transforms remove predictable structure along with non-stationarity.
The primary exception is variance stabilization. Log and Box-Cox transforms on heteroscedastic data improve accuracy for most model families by addressing a genuine modeling assumption violation without removing the signal.
These findings argue for complementing theoretical guidance with empirical validation in transformation selection for forecasting pipelines. Rather than asking “is the series stationary?”, practitioners should ask “does this transformation improve out-of-sample accuracy for my specific model and data?”
Future work could extend this analysis in several directions: evaluating deep learning forecasters (transformers, LSTMs) which may interact differently with preprocessing; testing on highly persistent autoregressive processes where differencing may provide genuine benefit at short horizons; developing automated transformation selection frameworks that use held-out validation to choose preprocessing empirically; and expanding real-world validation across diverse domains such as finance, energy, and healthcare to assess generalizability.
