Abstract
Near-term quantum computers are accessed through repeated circuit executions, which produce finite measurement records rather than exact deterministic outputs. In quantum reservoir computing, these records are converted into feature vectors for a classical readout. The standard expectation-value approach averages all shots from one labeled time step into a single feature vector. This reduces finite-shot noise, but it also gives the readout only one training example from many circuit executions. We introduce split-ensemble training: the same shots are split into groups, and each group average is used as a separate, partially denoised feature vector for the same target. The quantum circuit, task, and measurement budget remain unchanged. Across simulated forecasting benchmarks and real hardware experiments, this simple reorganization improves prediction when full averaging leaves the readout with too few training examples, with the strongest gains observed on hardware. Our results establish shot-record organization as a simple, broadly applicable algorithmic lever for improving near-term quantum learning without additional quantum hardware cost.
I
Introduction
Near-term quantum learning is limited not only by circuit design and hardware noise, but also by the finite way in which quantum systems are observed. A quantum circuit does not expose an exact feature vector in a single execution. Instead, it must be run repeatedly, producing a finite shot record that is later converted into data for a learning algorithm. This conversion step is often treated as a technical detail. In this paper, we show that it can directly affect predictive performance.
We study this effect in quantum reservoir computing (QRC), where a fixed quantum dynamical system generates nonlinear temporal features and only a classical readout is trained
[
fujii2017harnessing
,
nakajima2019boosting
,
mujal2021opportunities
,
kubota2022noise
,
palacios2024role
]

On real hardware, the readout observes neither an exact reservoir state nor a noiseless feature vector. The standard expectation-value (EV) protocol averages all shots at a time step into one feature vector. This suppresses sampling noise, but it also makes many costly circuit executions contribute only one training example. Raw stacking makes the opposite choice: every shot becomes a separate example. It gives the readout many examples, but each one is maximally noisy, which can attenuate fitted coefficients and flatten predictions
[
fuller1987measurement
,
carroll2006measurement
]
. Under the same quantum budget, EV and Raw are therefore opposite compromises: few clean examples versus many noisy ones.

,

. Existing work has studied reservoir capacity, finite-dimensional dynamics, input transformations, Hilbert-space resources, measurement protocols, and noise or dissipation as computational resources
[

,
martinezpena2023finite
,
mujal2023timeseries

,

fry2023noiseinduced

. Our work is complementary to these directions: rather than changing the reservoir or the measured observables, we study the classical organization of the finite measurement record after execution.
Finite-shot effects are increasingly treated as a core limitation of quantum learning. Hu
et al.
[
hu2023tackling
]
analyze how finite sampling noise limits the expressive capacity of physical and quantum learning systems. Kreplin and Roth
[
kreplin2024reduction
]
study finite-sampling noise in quantum neural networks and reduce it through variance regularization, while Moussa
et al.
[
moussa2023resource
]
treat shots as a limited resource during variational QML training. Recent work also studies generalization when both the number of training examples and the number of output measurement shots are finite
[
hu2024generalization
]
. In QRC specifically, Ahmed, Tennie, and Magri
[
ahmed2025optimal
]
address finitely sampled reservoirs through noise-aware regularization after expectation-value features have been formed. These works show that measurement economy is not a secondary implementation detail, but part of the learning problem. Our contribution acts at an earlier stage: before modifying the estimator or allocating additional shots, we ask whether the same measured data can be organized into more useful readout features.
The closest precedent is the partitioning heuristic of Cemin
et al.
[
cemin2024channels
]
for learning effective quantum channels on NISQ hardware. Their work uses shot subsets to obtain additional noisy estimates of quantum states. We build on this general idea but study it as a primary design variable for finite-shot QRC under a fixed quantum budget. In particular, we connect shot grouping to the readout’s effective data regime, compare it against duplication and noise-aware controls, and test the mechanism across benchmark regimes, reservoir architectures, and real hardware.
The resulting trade-off also connects to classical statistics. Using very noisy feature measurements can attenuate fitted coefficients
[
fuller1987measurement
,
carroll2006measurement
]

[

,
bai2010spectral
,
hsu2012random
,
dobriban2018high

VII
Discussion
The main implication is that finite-shot quantum learning has an algorithmic degree of freedom after measurement. A shot record is not automatically a training set; it becomes one only after a classical organization step. In QRC this step is especially visible, because the quantum reservoir is fixed and the readout learns from the measured features. The way these features are assembled can therefore change the learning problem without changing the quantum experiment.
This also clarifies why the effect is not just data augmentation. If the gain came only from giving the readout more examples, duplicating the EV feature would have been sufficient. It was not. The useful object is not an extra copy of the same averaged feature, but a distinct partially averaged view of the same measured quantum response. The regime dependence supports the same interpretation: grouping is useful when EV is too compressed for the readout, and it fades when EV already provides enough clean feature vectors.
The practical value of this observation is its low cost. split-ensemble requires no new circuit, no additional shots, no hardware modification, and no different readout architecture. It can be tested in classical post-processing whenever the shot record is available. A natural workflow is therefore simple: compute
ρ

This places the method next to, rather than in competition with, existing finite-shot approaches. Noise-aware QRC training regularizes after expectation-value features have been formed

.
The claims should be read in the setting studied here: repeated-measurement QRC, compact fixed reservoirs, scalar forecasting tasks, and linear ridge readouts. We do not claim a universal group size or a general quantum advantage. The transferable idea is more specific: whenever repeated measurements are converted into classical learning features, the organization of those measurements can matter. Natural extensions include adaptive group sizes, nonlinear readouts, richer observables, larger reservoirs, and related repeated-measurement settings such as quantum kernels, Born machines, or hybrid generative models.
VIII
Conclusion
We introduced split-ensemble training, a simple and budget-fair method for turning one finite quantum measurement record into multiple partially denoised feature views. The method changes neither the circuit, the task, nor the quantum measurement budget; it changes only the classical organization of the shots before readout training.
Across the studied QRC settings, this reorganization consistently improves prediction in the regimes where finite-shot learning is most constrained. The effect persists as the reservoir grows, becomes especially pronounced on real hardware, and is not reproduced by simple duplication or lightweight noise-aware corrections. This makes split-ensemble a practical tool for improving near-term QRC without requiring better devices, deeper circuits, or more quantum executions.
More broadly, our results show that measurement records are not passive outputs of quantum hardware. They are structured learning resources. For near-term quantum models, how we organize the shots can be as important as how we collect them.
