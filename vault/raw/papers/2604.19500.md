Connecting the forward problem to the inverse problem in uncertainty quantification of Earth system models using fast emulators
Abstract
Quantifying and reducing uncertainty in Earth system model parameterizations is essential to improving their reliability in decision-making.
Forward uncertainty propagation is used to derive parameter sensitivity but requires physically plausible parameter distributions first be learned from observations.
Bayesian inference offers a principled approach but can become ill-posed when observations weakly constrain parameters–a condition difficult to know prior to inference.
Addressing this gap, we show that parameter sensitivity results from forward uncertainty quantification can guide a non-iterative strategy for identifying observations informative to Bayesian calibration.
We explore both forward and inverse uncertainty quantification for parameterizations of atmospheric turbulence in the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model.
To overcome the computational bottleneck of

10
5
)

model evaluations required for both analyses, we leverage Gaussian process emulators trained on several hundred WRF simulations.
Using these emulators, we conduct a global sensitivity analysis across observation space, investigating how parameter contributions to output variance depend on quantity of interest, atmospheric stability, time-averaging length, and spatial location.
We then introduce nondimensional diagnostic measures that systematically identify regions where a parameter’s contribution to output variance exceeds observational noise and its independent effect exceeds interaction effects.
We demonstrate that observations from these regions serve as a strong proxy for accurate Bayesian calibration and reduced posterior uncertainty.
Through emulator-aided Bayesian inversion with synthetic observations, we show how parameter uncertainty can be systematically reduced by leveraging sensitivity information.
Because this framework precedes inference, it provides a practical, non-iterative approach to improving the observational efficiency of Bayesian learning in chaotic dynamical systems.

Plain Language Summary
Climate and weather models rely on submodels with fixed parameters that can affect their predictions.
Many of these parameters have traditionally been hand-tuned using limited field data or scientific insight, and their uncertainties are therefore not well known.
Reducing these uncertainties matters, but it is hard to know ahead of time whether the available data will tell us enough about the parameters we care about.
Here, we develop a method for choosing data that are most useful for learning uncertain parameters.
To make this computationally practical, we use artificial intelligence to imitate a submodel for turbulence, allowing us to explore many parameter combinations with only a few hundred model runs instead of millions.
We then examine how changes in the parameter values affect model predictions.
This helps reveal how parameter effects differ across atmospheric variables, atmospheric conditions, across space, and across different averaging times.
Using this information, we identify scenarios where the effects of parameters are strong enough to stand out from observational noise and where the influence of different parameters can be distinguished from one another.
In a proof-of-concept test using idealized simulations, we observe that parameters with smaller uncertainty learned from data match those expected by our analysis.

Introduction
Earth system models (ESMs) simulate the Earth’s physical processes by solving the governing equations of fluid motion, coupled with biological, chemical, and other physical processes, on a computational grid.
These models make predictions of weather and climate systems, urban flows, pollutant dispersion in complex terrain, and atmosphere–renewable-energy interactions
[
salamanca2011study
,
bauer2015quiet
,
veers2019grand
,
dunbar2021calibration
]
.
When processes, which are multi-scale in space and time, fall below the grid scales, submodels, referred to as
parameterizations
, represent the effects of subgrid-scale processes to the grid-scale processes using a combination of empirical and physics-based relationships and empirically calibrated parameters
[
stensrud2007parameterization
]
.
A major source of error and uncertainty in parameterizations are these calibrated parameters
[
stensrud2007parameterization
,
hourdin2017art
,
lamb2026perspectives
]
.
The parameters are often tuned to sparse field observations or idealized simulation data, so they are widely acknowledged to be state-dependent and non-universal.
Quantifying and reducing uncertainty in these parameters is essential to improve predictive accuracy and reliability of ESMs for various applications.
Efforts to quantify uncertainty in empirical parameters have followed two statistical paradigms: the forward (frequentist) approach and the inverse (Bayesian) approach.
The forward approach propagates parameter values through the model to quantify uncertainty in resulting model outputs, which can then be apportioned to the different parameters.
Therefore, in the forward approach, parameter uncertainty is treated as an input assumption, and its influence is quantified through sensitivity analysis.
This has been the classical approach used in previous studies to study uncertainty in ESM parameterizations.
For example,
\citeA
nielsen2010evaluation examined parameter observability and its dependence on observation characteristics,
\citeA
yang2017sensitivity linked parametric sensitivity in planetary boundary layer and surface-layer schemes to terrain and atmospheric stability, and
\citeA
baki2022determining and
\citeA
pathak2020uncertainty applied global sensitivity analysis to identify key parameters across various parameterization schemes.
These studies demonstrate how forward UQ can reveal parameter sensitivity and calibration potential, but they do not determine physically plausible parameter intervals, which must instead be inferred from observations.
The Bayesian inverse approach to UQ provides a principled framework for accounting for observations in a probabilistic manner through posterior distributions over parameters
[
kennedy2001bayesian
]
.
This UQ approach has become increasingly popular with the growing availability of data and computational power, and has been applied across multiple scales of Earth system modeling, from climate models
[
dunbar2021calibration
,
howland2022parameter
]
to turbulence
[
edeling2014bayesian
,
souza2020uncertainty
,
shin2026accelerated
]
and cloud microphysics parameterizations
[
kim2025uncertainty
]
.
Yet with a purely Bayesian approach, calibration can become ill-posed if parameters are not well-constrained by the observations or structural model-observation discrepancy dominates
[
hourdin2017art
]
.
This issue is typically diagnosed
a posteriori
to the inference process and is directly tied to the observations used for inference.
Therefore, observations play a critical role in the characterization of parameter uncertainty.
Observation selection has been addressed using frameworks such as Bayesian optimal experimental design, in which observations are chosen to maximize the expected information gain about uncertain parameters
[
chaloner1995bayesian
]
.
These approaches are formulated using information-theoretic design criteria, such as expected information gain, mutual information or entropy reduction
[
dunbar2022ensemble
,
huan2024optimal
]
.
However, methods based on this framework often rely on iterative and computationally intensive optimization procedures and, in practice, have seen limited adoption due to computational bottlenecks, a lack of standardization, and a focus on methodological development rather than application-specific needs
[
rainforth2024modern
]
.
The application of optimal experimental design to the chaotic dynamical atmospheric system is challenging because the informativeness of observations depends on the quantity of interest and on the multi-scale, spatiotemporal variability of the high-dimensional chaotic atmospheric state.
Furthermore, the multi-objective nature of atmospheric modeling and the exhaustive combinatorial search over possible observation subsets makes the resulting optimization computationally infeasible.
As a result, the questions of which variables to observe, how they should be averaged, and where and when observations should be collected to calibrate ESM parameterizations remain critical but largely unanswered.
More generally, the efficient selection of data to improve models is core to model learning across multiple scientific communities
[
reeves1999sequential
,
huan2024optimal
,
lamb2026perspectives
]
.
In this study, we propose a framework in which global sensitivity analysis based on forward uncertainty propagation guides a non-iterative selection of observations to be used in the Bayesian inverse problem of calibrating and quantifying uncertainty in parameters in a chaotic dynamical system.
To demonstrate the framework, we apply it to parameterizations of turbulence in an atmospheric model.
With the aid of machine-learning emulators, both the forward and inverse uncertainty quantification methods are conducted using only
𝒪
​
(
10
2
)
\mathcal{O}(10^{2})
evaluations of the atmospheric model, in contrast to
𝒪
​
(
10
5
)
\mathcal{O}(10^{5})
model evaluations required in standard sampling-based statistical analysis approaches
[
geyer2011introduction
]
.
First, by computing sensitivity indices over the observation space, we show that global sensitivity analysis reveals how parameter sensitivity to key quantities vary across atmospheric stability regimes, time-averaging windows, and spatial observation locations.
Second, we introduce nondimensional diagnostic measures that leverage sensitivity indices to identify regions in the observation space where the parameter signal on model output is stronger than observational noise and the parameter’s independent effect is stronger than its interaction effects.
Criteria based on the diagnostic measures enables us to quantify which quantities should be measured, where they should be measured, and how they should be time-averaged in order to construct informative observations of the chaotic dynamical system.
By performing Markov-chain Monte Carlo sampling with the emulators, we show that the diagnostic measures serve as a strong proxy for accurate Bayesian parameter calibration and reduced posterior parameter uncertainty.
Systematic observation selection prior to the inference process improves observational efficiency in Bayesian inference, which is a central topic in inverse problems, parameter estimation, and data assimilation.
Because the statistical methods introduced in this study are not specific to ESM parameterizations, they apply to UQ of models in chaotic dynamical systems more broadly, and are therefore expected to extend beyond the present context to other disciplines.

Methodology
This section outlines the methods used in this study.
First, we detail (1) the global sensitivity analysis to quantify parameter contributions to model-output variance based on forward uncertainty propagation, (2) the Bayesian inverse approach to quantify parameter uncertainty based on observations, (3) the machine-learning emulator that enables both statistical analyses at a feasible computational cost, and (4) a framework that leverages results of the global sensitivity analysis to guide the selection of informative observations for Bayesian inversion.
Then, we describe the atmospheric model, the target parameterizations, and the simulation cases used to generate model outputs and synthetic observations for testing the proposed observation selection framework.

2.2
Observation selection framework
In the chaotic atmospheric system, the informativeness of observations varies across space and time within a high-dimensional state space.
While Bayesian optimal experimental design provides a principled way to account for observational uncertainty and select informative measurements, the inherently multi-objective nature of atmospheric modeling and the combinatorial search over possible measurement subsets makes the resulting optimization often computationally infeasible in practice.

2.2.1
Limitations of the framework
The nondimensional diagnostic measures inherit assumptions from the ANOVA decomposition used in global sensitivity analysis.
Their reliability depends on the accuracy of the statistical estimators, which in this study is determined by (i) the number of emulator samples used to compute the estimators and (ii) the number of model evaluations used to train the emulator.
Furthermore, statistical analysis of the parameter-model-output mapping is of an interpolative nature and is not guaranteed to generalize outside the sampled parameter space.
Lastly, in practice, the overlap region identified by the diagnostic measures may be empty or may result in multiple disjoint regions, reflecting competing trade-offs in calibrating multiple parameters.

To summarize, the results of emulator-aided global sensitivity analysis elucidate the sensitivity of the model to parameters in coupled physics parameterizations across multiple factors important to characterizing model predictions of the chaotic dynamical system.
Accurate statistical analysis of the forward mapping from parameter space to model-output space is made possible by lightweight, modular ML emulators.
The analysis reveals which parameters dominate, where they act across the model-output space, and how they interact across different timescales.
It also reveals that some parameter interactions persist under long temporal averaging; these interactions should be examined further to determine whether they reflect, or in some cases reveal, underlying physical mechanisms in the flow.
This suggests a direction for future work to apply this method to investigate nonlinear interactions among parameters and across multiple coupled physics parameterizations.
Although the benefits of the sensitivity analysis are clear, the forward propagation of uncertainty is not a substitute for inverse UQ, since characterizing physically meaningful credible bounds for parameters requires Bayesian inversion using observations.
3.2
Observation selection for Bayesian inversion
The selection of observations in a chaotic dynamical system is particularly relevant to assimilating field observations for parameterization development in ESMs.
Previous results in Sect.
3.1
showed that the parameter sensitivity varies significantly with factors that characterize model predictions.
In the subsequent results, we explore how each of these factors, when used to construct observations that drive inverse UQ, influences the inferred parameter uncertainty.
In doing so, we explore how parameter sensitivity can be leveraged using the diagnostic measures introduced in Sect.
2.2
to calibrate and reduce uncertainty in PBL and SFCLAY scheme parameters.
As a demonstration of the potential in this study, we use synthetic observations (see Sect.

Conclusions
The current study demonstrates an efficient framework, aided by machine-learning emulators, that connects the forward problem to the inverse problem of UQ using diagnostic measures of sensitivity indices for parameter calibration in ESM parameterizations.
We introduced that the framework informs a non-iterative selection of observations considering multiple factors in constructing informative observations for a chaotic dynamical system.
In doing so, we showed that modular, lightweight ML emulators make it practical to apply standard statistical analyses to gain physical insight into ESM parameterizations and use that insight to guide their calibration.
Global sensitivity analysis of parameters provides a quantitative interpretation of how the model represents planetary boundary-layer physics, since the calibrated parameters correspond to physically meaningful processes governing turbulent momentum and heat transport.
We revealed how the parameter sensitivities depend on the output quantity of interest, time-averaging window, atmospheric stability regime, and spatial locations.
For wind speed, parameters controlling turbulent production and dissipation–such as the profile-shape exponent
p
p
in the YSU PBL scheme–primarily influence the region near the PBL height.
In contrast, the roughness length
z
0
z_{0}
, which enters the PBL scheme through surface fluxes via the surface-layer parameterization, mainly affects the surface layer and, depending on the stability regime, the flow aloft.
For potential temperature, the effect of
z
0
z_{0}
decreases as parameters relevant to buoyancy such as the countergradient term constant
b
b
increase in contribution.
Parameter sensitivities are more strongly affected by temporal averaging in the convective regime than in the stable regime, likely due to the larger turbulence scales of convective flow.
Recognizing how these factors that are relevant to the chaotic dynamical system interact can inform how model parameters should be calibrated using observations.
Additionally, the interaction-effect sensitivities of parameters create an opportunity for a more systematic exploration of parameter interactions arising from coupled physics parameterizations in ESMs.
In addition to revealing parameter sensitivities of the model, we introduced a framework that leverages information from the sensitivity analysis to systematically and non-iteratively guiding the selection of observations for Bayesian model calibration of ESM parameterizations.
We assert that the parameter sensitivity projected onto the observation space is the primary indicator for the informativeness an observation for parameter calibration.
This motivates our framework which uses nondimensional diagnostic measures of sensitivity indices to identify regions in the observation space with relatively low observational noise and weak parameter interaction effects, prior to the inference process.
This possibility of a non-iterative selection of informative observations provides an efficient pathway to improving observational efficiency in Bayesian learning.
By performing emulator-aided Bayesian inversion with synthetic observations for which the ground-truth parameter values are known, we demonstrated how the uncertainty across the coupled YSU PBL-revised MM5 scheme parameters can be systematically reduced by leveraging information on parameter sensitivities to the observed quantity of interest, atmospheric stability during observation, observation averaging time length, and the spatial location of the observations.
In doing so, we corroborated our assertion that observations are informative to parameters in regions of the observation space where the model is sensitive to those target parameters.
These findings are motivated by the core idea that the informativeness of an observation for model calibration is a useful measure of its value to the model.
Therefore, understanding the model is the best way to identify the most informative observations.
In this sense, the forward and inverse statistical paradigms are best viewed as complementary in model calibration, where insights from the forward approach can improve the observational efficiency of Bayesian model inversion.
We expect this perspective may be useful further parameterization development and efficient observation use for Earth system models.
Future work should consider the application of the observation selection framework in non-idealized simulations initialized and forced with reanalysis data over realistic terrain and surface conditions.
The framework should be evaluated in settings of surface heterogeneity, where the observation space expands from vertical locations to include horizontal locations to address questions on land-atmosphere interactions over surface heterogeneity in ESMs.
Furthermore, efforts to expand on the framework with an explicit treatment of the model-form or structural error are needed.
This will allow learning and development of parameterizations from a wider range of observations, such as field observations, turbulence-resolving simulations, etc.
Finally, the framework should be used to investigate the influence of parameters across the coupled physics parameterizations, including microphysics and radiation schemes, to elucidate phenomena arising from the complex, coupled turbulent dynamics of the atmosphere.
