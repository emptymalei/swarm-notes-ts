Abstract.
Accurate air traffic prediction in the terminal airspace (TA) is pivotal for proactive air traffic management (ATM). However, existing data-driven approaches predominantly rely on time series-based forecasting paradigms, which inherently overlook critical aircraft state information, such as real-time kinematics and proximity to airspace boundaries. To address this limitation, we propose
AeroSense
, a direct state-to-flow modeling framework for air traffic prediction. Unlike classical time series-based methods that first aggregate aircraft trajectories into macroscopic flow sequences before modeling, AeroSense explicitly represents the real-time airspace situation as
a dynamic set of aircraft states
, enabling the direct processing of a variable number of aircraft instead of time series as inputs. Specifically, we introduce a situation-aware state representation that enables AeroSense to sense the instantaneous terminal airspace situation directly from microscopic aircraft states. Furthermore, we design a model architecture that incorporates masked self-attention to capture inter-aircraft interactions, together with two decoupled prediction heads to model heterogeneous flow dynamics across two key functional areas of the TA. Extensive experiments on a large-scale real-world airport dataset demonstrate that AeroSense consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance, validating that direct modeling of microscopic aircraft states yields substantially higher predictive fidelity than time series-based baselines. Moreover, the proposed framework exhibits superior robustness during peak traffic periods, achieves Pareto-optimal performance under dayparting multi-object evaluation, and provides meaningful interpretability through attention-based visualizations.

Central to AeroSense is a

(1)

We propose AeroSense, a situation-aware modeling framework that establishes an end-to-end mapping from aircraft-level states to future regional traffic flows.
The framework naturally supports dynamic traffic density, eliminates the need for look-back windows, and avoids information loss caused by spatial or temporal aggregation.
(3)
We introduce a situation-aware state representation that encodes boundary interactions, kinematic trends, and control intent as inductive biases. This design enables the model to reason about traffic evolution from underlying flight dynamics rather than purely statistical correlations.
(4)
Extensive experiments on large-scale real-world terminal airspace data demonstrate that AeroSense consistently outperforms state-of-the-art time-series baselines, particularly under high-density and high-volatility traffic conditions, while providing physically interpretable predictions.

Effectiveness of decoupled prediction heads.
The

Impact of aggregation strategy.
A pivotal observation is the significant performance degradation with Mean or Max pooling, where AR MAE increases to 4.456 and 5.339, respectively. This underscores the structural misalignment between these operators and the task of volume estimation. Unlike classification tasks where features are often scale-invariant, traffic flow is an extensive quantity. Mean pooling acts as a normalization step that dilutes density information—rendering a sparse scenario indistinguishable from a congested one if kinematic trends are similar—while Max pooling isolates a single outlier and discards the contribution of background traffic. In contrast, Summation pooling functions as a cumulative operator.
By linearly aggregating the physics-augmented embeddings, it captures the cumulative traffic demand within the airspace, ensuring the global representation naturally reflects the total scale of the dynamic aircraft set.

Multi-Objective Dayparting Evaluation (RQ3)
Air traffic flows exhibit pronounced temporal heterogeneity, with dramatic
demand surges during morning and evening peak periods. To systematically
evaluate the robustness of AeroSense under such volatile conditions, we conduct
a dayparting evaluation by analyzing the temporal distribution of Mean
Absolute Error (MAE) over a complete 24-hour cycle. Specifically, the day is
partitioned into twelve non-overlapping 2-hour intervals, each treated as an
independent evaluation objective.
As shown in Figure
4
, AeroSense consistently achieves
low MAE across all time intervals for both the AP and AR areas. In particular,
AeroSense attains Pareto-optimal performance in the AR region
,
forming a dominant error frontier that is not surpassed by any baseline under
this multi-objective dayparting evaluation.
The advantage of AeroSense is especially pronounced during high-volatility
periods, such as the morning congestion peak (08:00–10:00). While
macroscopic time-series baselines (e.g., Autoformer and FEDformer) exhibit
substantial performance degradation with sharp error spikes, AeroSense maintains
a smooth and stable error profile. This robustness indicates that directly
modeling real-time microscopic aircraft states effectively mitigates the lag
effects inherent in aggregated historical statistics, enabling proactive
anticipation of traffic flow transitions rather than reactive forecasting based
on past trends.

(Guo

(Du

(Wang

6.
Conclusion
This paper revisits short-term air traffic flow prediction in the TA from a fundamentally different perspective. We argue that the
prevailing macroscopic paradigm—based on grid discretization or aggregated time
series—introduces an inherent mismatch between the learning representation and
the true physical state of air traffic, which is intrinsically a dynamic,
variable-cardinality set of aircraft evolving in continuous space.
To address this mismatch, we propose
AeroSense
, the first state-to-flow modeling framework that directly maps microscopic
aircraft states to macroscopic traffic flows. By explicitly representing the
instantaneous airspace situation as a dynamic set, AeroSense eliminates the
dependence on historical look-back windows and avoids information loss caused by
spatial or temporal aggregation. Central to the framework is a situation-aware state representation that encodes kinematic trends,
boundary proximity, and control intent as inductive biases, enabling
the model to reason about traffic evolution from flight dynamics.
Building upon this representation, AeroSense integrates masked self-attention
for modeling inter-aircraft interactions and task-decoupled
prediction heads to capture the heterogeneous flow dynamics of different functional regions within the TA.
Extensive experiments on a large-scale real-world terminal airspace dataset
demonstrate that AeroSense consistently outperforms state-of-the-art time-series baselines. The results confirm that directly modeling
microscopic aircraft states yields substantially higher predictive fidelity,
particularly under high-density and high-volatility traffic conditions. Beyond
average performance gains, AeroSense exhibits strong robustness during peak
periods and achieves Pareto-optimal performance under multi-objective dayparting evaluation,
highlighting its practical value for proactive air traffic management.
Moreover, attention-based visualizations reveal that the model learns
task-conditioned and physically interpretable interaction patterns, providing
transparent insights into how individual aircraft contribute to regional flow dynamics.
