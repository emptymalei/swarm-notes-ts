Introduction
Conformal prediction has emerged as a leading framework for distribution-free uncertainty quantification in machine learning
(Vovk
et al.
,
2005
; Shafer and Vovk,
2008
; Papadopoulos
et al.
,
2002
)
. By providing finite-sample coverage guarantees with minimal distributional assumptions, conformal methods ground predictions in rigorous statistical validity, a property increasingly demanded by AI governance frameworks. Recent tutorials
(Angelopoulos and Bates,
2023
)
have accelerated adoption, and extensions now address quantile regression
(Romano
et al.
,
2019
)
, distribution shift
(Tibshirani
et al.
,
2019
; Barber
et al.
,
2023
)
, and adaptive online settings
(Gibbs and Candès,
2021
)
. For survey-based social measurement, where predictions inform policy discussions about public attitudes, reliable uncertainty is not optional: prediction sets that over-cover some demographic groups while under-covering others undermine the scientific basis for equitable policy.
Standard split conformal prediction guarantees
marginal
coverage: the prediction set contains the true response with probability at least
1
−
α
1-\alpha
on average over the test population. However, this aggregate guarantee can mask substantial disparities across demographic subgroups. In complex surveys with design-based weights and intersectional demographic cells, thin calibration cells (some with fewer than 50 observations) create settings where marginal validity and subgroup reliability diverge sharply.
The canonical theoretical remedy is Mondrian conformal prediction
(Vovk
et al.
,
2005
; Vovk,
2012
)
, which estimates group-specific thresholds to achieve conditional coverage within each predefined subgroup.
Romano
et al.
(
2020
)
formalize equalized coverage objectives, and
Ding
et al.
(
2023
)
extend Mondrian calibration to the many-class regime, showing that clustered thresholds can recover conditional validity when individual class cells are thin. More recent work proposes adaptive group selection for equalized coverage
(Zhou and Sesia,
2024
)
, bridges fairness and efficiency through surrogate-assisted clustering
(Gao
et al.
,
2025
)
, and studies multi-group learning for conformal validity
(Deng
et al.
,
2023
)
. These advances notwithstanding, conditional validity beyond predefined groups faces fundamental impossibility results
(Lei and Wasserman,
2014
; Foygel Barber
et al.
,
2021
)
, motivating alternative approaches through multivalid
(Jung
et al.
,
2023
; Hébert-Johnson

,
2018
)
and conditional
(Gibbs
et al.
,
2025
)
guarantees. Critically,
Cresswell

(
2024
)
demonstrate that conformal prediction sets can themselves cause disparate impact, underscoring that nominal fairness corrections may introduce new equity concerns.
Despite this theoretical progress, the empirical behavior of Mondrian conformal prediction in realistic survey settings remains poorly characterized. In such settings, calibration cells may contain as few as 32 observations, outcome distributions vary across groups, and design-based weights create effective sample sizes smaller than nominal counts. Whether Mondrian’s theoretical benefits translate into practical fairness improvements under these conditions is an open question.
Our empirical setting centers on public attitudes toward artificial intelligence, a domain where demographic variation in trust and perceived impact is well documented
(Zhang and Dafoe,
2019
; Brauner
et al.
,
2023
; Novozhilova
et al.
,
2024
)
. Trust in AI systems varies across age, education, and racial groups
(Shin,
2021
; Glikson and Woolley,
2020
; Neudert
et al.
,
2020
)
, making intersectional subgroup analysis essential for responsible deployment of predictive models in this space. Ordinal response scales are standard in survey measurement
(Agresti,
2010
; McCullagh,
1980
)
, and cumulative distribution function-based nonconformity scores extend conformal prediction to ordered outcomes
(Lu
et al.
,
2022
; Gupta
et al.
,
2022
)
. Risk-controlling prediction sets
(Bates
et al.
,
2021
)
provide a general framework for structured losses. Complex survey designs require design-based weights for population-representative inference
(Lumley,
2010
)
, and conformal prediction under covariate shift
(Tibshirani
et al.
,
2019
)
provides the closest theoretical connection, though formal guarantees for survey-weighted conformal calibration remain an open problem. The trade-off between fairness criteria is well established
(Chouldechova,
2017
; Kleinberg
et al.
,
2017
)
, and intersectional approaches
(Kearns
et al.
,
2018
)
together with calibration-fairness connections
(Pleiss
et al.
,
2017
)
inform our subgroup analysis.
We address this gap with a 100-split empirical study of ordinal conformal prediction on the Pew American Trends Panel (Wave 152,

RQ1.
Does standard conformal prediction achieve nominal marginal coverage?
RQ2.
Do subgroup disparities persist under standard conformal prediction?
RQ3.

RQ4.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide a 100-split verified empirical study of ordinal conformal prediction in a complex survey setting with weighted subgroup evaluation, showing that nominal marginal validity coexists with meaningful subgroup disparities across race

We have shown that standard split CP achieves nominal marginal coverage while masking subgroup disparities (
