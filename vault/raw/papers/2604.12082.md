Introduction
Grid-scale battery energy storage systems (BESS) have become
central to European electricity market operations, with
prequalified FCR capacity in Germany growing by 180 MW in 2024
alone, and aFRR prequalifications increasing by over 400%
year-on-year
(Regelleistung.net,
2024
)
.
As these assets compete across multiple simultaneous markets
— frequency containment reserve (FCR), automatic frequency
restoration reserve (aFRR), day-ahead (DA), and continuous
intraday (XBID) — their commercial viability increasingly
depends on automated, forecast-driven optimization systems.
However, the effectiveness of such systems depends critically
on how forecast information is translated into economic
decisions.
Recent work has advanced multi-market
BESS
optimization
substantially.
Schaurecker
et al.
(
2025
)
demonstrate that a dynamic programming
formulation of the rolling intrinsic strategy captures
near-optimal intraday trading profits.
Seifert
et al.
(
2024
)
develop a stochastic multi-market
bidding framework based on
SDDP
for coordinated
participation across
FCR
, day-ahead, and intraday
markets, finding that coordination does not necessarily
improve revenues and that
FCR
often dominates
optimal strategies.
Hornek and others (
2025
)
compare forecast-driven and perfect-foresight
strategies on the continuous intraday market, finding that a
forecast-driven system captures approximately 90% of
perfect-foresight revenue (achieving revenues only about
11% below perfect foresight).
A parallel literature has begun to examine the mismatch between
forecast accuracy and economic value.
Uniejewski and others (
2025
)
demonstrate that
MAE
and
RMSE
are only weakly correlated with
BESS
profits in the
context of day-ahead electricity markets and
BESS
arbitrage, capturing only a limited dimension of forecast
performance.
Vanderschueren
et al.
(
2022
)
show more broadly that the decision
strategy dominates the training strategy in cost-sensitive
optimization, and
Smets
et al.
(
2024
)
propose decision-focused
training for
BESS
market participation.
Building on the foundational work of
Morales
et al.
(
2013
)
on
forecast value in energy systems, this literature increasingly
recognizes that statistical accuracy and economic value diverge.
Several of these contributions are recent preprints that
have not yet completed peer review; we include them because
they represent the current frontier of this rapidly
developing literature.
Yet two gaps remain.
First, the continuous intraday market is structurally more
ordinal than day-ahead auctions: the
DP
dispatch problem
requires only the
relative ranking
of future prices, not
their absolute levels.
No existing study has exploited this structural property to
identify a formal metric that predicts decision quality in
intraday
BESS
trading.
Second, the literature is almost exclusively focused on the
German market; the Swiss balancing market—structurally
distinct due to its hydro-dominated reserve provision—remains
entirely unexplored.
As a result, it remains unclear whether improvements in
statistical forecast accuracy translate into improved economic
outcomes in real trading systems, and how market-specific
structural drivers shape revenue in non-German markets.
This paper makes three contributions.
First, and most importantly, we identify a formal
tau-sufficiency region
(
τ
∗
≈
0.85
\tau^{*}\approx 0.85
–
0.95
0.95
)
in the continuous intraday market, beyond which value capture
saturates near its theoretical maximum regardless of further
improvements in forecast accuracy.
While
Uniejewski and others (
2025
)
demonstrate that rank-based metrics
better predict
BESS
profits than
MAE
in day-ahead
markets, we identify a quantified
threshold
—not merely
a correlation—and show that this threshold is stable across
market regimes, volatility levels, and subperiods.
The structural explanation is that the
DP
dispatch problem
is inherently ordinal: it depends on the relative ordering of
prices rather than their absolute values
(Morales
et al.
,
2013
)
.
Second, we demonstrate that revenue in multi-market
BESS
systems is primarily driven by capacity allocation rather than
intraday forecast quality.
Under reserve market constraints (
PICASSO
compliance),
the residual
SoC
budget allocated to
XBID
yields
revenues substantially lower than
FCR
per
MW
committed—not because intraday arbitrage is structurally less
profitable, but because co-participation in reserve markets
physically limits the available intraday cycling capacity.
We find no evidence of systematic misallocation of capacity
across markets within the scope of our model and dataset
under realistic operating conditions.
Third, we provide the first empirical analysis of the Swiss
balancing market using real Swissgrid data, showing that
hydrological conditions are strongly associated with balancing
prices and battery revenues, highlighting the importance of
market-specific structural drivers absent from existing
German-focused literature.

Section

Section

practical implications, and Section
6

Market Structure and Data
2.1
Market Architecture
Battery storage systems in Central Europe participate in a
cascade of sequential markets, each with distinct gate closures,
clearing mechanisms, and revenue structures.
We consider four markets in Germany and their Swiss equivalents.
The
FCR

TSOs under the FCR Cooperation framework.
Participants submit capacity bids; accepted bids receive a
pay-as-bid capacity payment with no separate energy settlement.
In Germany, the minimum bid size is 1 MW and bids must be
symmetric (equal upward and downward capacity).
Gate closure is D

Accepted capacity bids receive a capacity payment; energy

Under

minimum of 30 minutes (

The Day-Ahead (

−

Second, the

The Swiss reserve pool is approximately 400 MW, supplied
predominantly by flexible hydroelectric generation.
When reservoir levels are anomalously high relative to

5
Discussion
The three empirical findings of Section
4
—
the tau-sufficiency threshold, the
FCR
dominance under
reserve constraints, and the Swiss hydro transmission
mechanism—jointly reframe how
BESS
operators should
evaluate forecast quality and allocate system capacity.
We discuss their theoretical and practical implications in turn.
5.1
Theoretical Implication
The tau-sufficiency result has a theoretical implication that
extends beyond battery storage applications.
The
DP
Rolling Intrinsic algorithm is a specific instance
of a broader class of optimization problems in which the
objective function depends on the relative ordering of input
parameters rather than their absolute values.
A broad class of such problems—including certain forms of
inventory management, scheduling, and trading—share this
ordinal structure: the optimal policy is determined by the
ranking of future states, not their precise magnitude.
For any such problem, standard pointwise error metrics are
misaligned with decision quality by construction, and rank
correlation metrics are more appropriate evaluation criteria.
This connects to the emerging literature on decision-focused
learning
(Smets
et al.
,
2024
; Vanderschueren
et al.
,
2022
)
, which proposes
retraining forecast models to minimize downstream decision cost
rather than statistical error.
While decision-focused learning has been shown to improve
out-of-sample profits for energy storage systems, albeit
at the cost of significant computational complexity
(Smets
et al.
,
2024
)
, this approach integrates the downstream
objective into model training rather than characterizing
the decision structure itself.
Our finding is complementary but distinct: we show that for
problems with an ordinal decision structure, the alignment
between statistical metrics and decision quality can be empirically
characterized by a single threshold in rank correlation.
This threshold is a property of the decision problem, not of
the forecasting method, and is therefore stable across forecast
architectures.
For tau-sufficient forecasts, decision-focused retraining may
offer limited additional value—a result that simplifies the
operational pipeline for
BESS
operators.
Consistent with
Vanderschueren
et al.
(
2022
)
, who show
empirically that decision-making strategies have a larger
impact on cost performance than the objective function
used for training, our results highlight that improvements
in the decision framework can outweigh gains from
modifying the predictive model itself.
5.2
Practical Implications for BESS Operators
The findings translate into three concrete operational
recommendations.
First, forecast evaluation for intraday dispatch should
prioritize rank-based metrics over pointwise error metrics
such as
MAE
or
RMSE
.
The relevant question for a
BESS
operator is not
“what is my forecast error?” but “does my forecast preserve
the ranking of intraday price opportunities?”
In our setting, forecasts achieving
τ

not materially improve dispatch revenue.
Second, investment in forecast improvement should prioritize
Layer 1 over Layer 3.
Our attribution results show that the capacity allocation

Improving the cross-market spread forecast that informs

when the static

Third, operators in hydro-dependent markets should incorporate
reservoir level monitoring into their bid strategy.
The Swiss evidence shows that hydrological anomalies are

A simple regime-switching strategy that raises bid percentiles

significant fraction of this opportunity.
5.3
Market Regime and Heterogeneity
The findings should be interpreted in the context of the
German and Swiss markets over 2020–2025.
Two sources of heterogeneity deserve attention.
The
FCR
dominance result depends on the current
structure of the German balancing market, where
BESS
penetration in
FCR
has grown rapidly but has not yet
saturated to the point of driving capacity prices to zero.
As
BESS
capacity continues to expand,
FCR
prices
may decline as
BESS
penetration increases, compressing
the
FCR

XBID

The tau-sufficiency result, by contrast, is structural: it
depends on the ordinal nature of the
DP
dispatch
problem, not on the relative profitability of
FCR
and
XBID

The Swiss hydro finding highlights the importance of
market-specific structural drivers that are absent from
German-focused models.
Similar mechanisms may operate in other hydro-dependent
European markets—Austria, Norway, and Iberia—where
hydroelectric generation plays a dominant role in reserve
provision.
Operators and researchers working in these markets should
account for hydrological conditions as a primary revenue
driver for downward balancing services, rather than treating
balancing prices as purely driven by gas and demand dynamics.
5.4
Limitations and Scope
Three limitations of this study should be acknowledged.
First,
aFRR
energy activations are modeled via
expected value and
SoC
buffer constraints rather
than fully dynamic real-time simulation.
This simplification is expected to be conservative for
revenue estimation: the PICASSO buffer is calibrated to
worst-case activation scenarios, so any dynamic
activation exceeding the buffer would reduce available
XBID
capacity and bias revenue estimates downward
rather than upward.
However, the dynamic coupling between
aFRR
activations and
XBID
decisions is not fully
modeled.
In practice, concurrent activations reduce
XBID
capacity in real time, creating interactions that the
layer-separated architecture does not capture.
This may result in occasional infeasibility in
high-activation periods that the static buffer approach
does not detect.
Second,
XBID

15-minute aggregated prices from SMARD, abstracting from
order book dynamics, bid-ask spreads, and partial fills.
Schaurecker
et al.
(
2025
)
demonstrate that order-book-level
execution yields substantially higher intraday revenues than
aggregated-price models, implying that our
XBID
revenue
estimates are conservative.
The
FCR
dominance ratio of 6.5
×
\times
would be closer
to 2–4
×
\times
under optimal intraday execution, as discussed
in Section
4.2
.
Third, the tau-sufficiency threshold
τ
∗
≈
0.85
\tau^{*}\approx 0.85
–
0.95
0.95
is estimated on German
XBID
data for 2023–2025.
While the existence of a threshold is a structural
property of ordinal decision problems, its precise
numerical value may vary across markets with different
price dynamics, liquidity structures, or trading
horizons.
Validation on other European markets would refine the
quantitative estimate of
τ
∗

and strengthen the
generalizability of the finding.
6
Conclusion
This paper examines the relationship between forecast accuracy
and decision quality in multi-market battery storage
optimization, using six years of real market data from Germany
and Switzerland.
Three findings emerge.
First, we identify an empirical tau-sufficiency region
(
τ
∗
≈
0.85
\tau^{*}\approx 0.85
–
0.95
0.95
) in Kendall rank correlation
beyond which value capture saturates near 100% of oracle
revenue, with limited sensitivity to further improvements in
absolute forecast error.
The
DP
dispatch problem is inherently ordinal—it
depends on the relative ranking of prices, not their absolute
levels—and any forecast that sufficiently preserves this
ranking induces a near-optimal policy.
Standard accuracy metrics such as
MAE
fail to capture
this property, leading to systematic misalignment between
statistical evaluation and economic performance.
Second, in multi-market systems under reserve constraints,
the capacity allocation decision of Layer 1 is the primary
driver of total revenue.
FCR
capacity is endogenously saturated by the PICASSO
buffer constraint in all observed weeks of our sample, making
the Layer 1 allocation—not the intraday forecast—the
economically dominant decision.
The tau-sufficiency result completes the picture: for the
intraday budget that remains after reserve obligations,
standard
ML
forecasts already achieve tau-sufficiency,
making further forecast improvement of limited incremental
value for intraday dispatch.
Third, the Swiss balancing market provides one of the first
empirical documentations of the hydro-balancing market
transmission mechanism: hydrological surplus anomalies are
significantly associated with balancing market prices
(Spearman
p
=
0.0002
p=0.0002
) and revenue (OLS
p
=
0.0005
p=0.0005
),
with high-hydro weeks generating
approximately 8
×
\times
higher downward reserve revenue than
low-hydro weeks.
This finding highlights the importance of market-specific
structural drivers that are absent from German-focused models
and relevant for
BESS
operators in hydro-dependent
markets across Europe.
Together, these results reframe the forecast evaluation
problem for
BESS
operators.
The relevant question is not “what is my
MAE
?” but
“does my forecast achieve tau-sufficiency, and is my
capacity allocation strategy adapted to the current market
regime?”
Rank correlation metrics and regime-aware bidding, rather
than pointwise accuracy and static strategies, are the
primary tools that deliver economic value in heterogeneous
European balancing markets.

HMM+SAC Adaptive Bidding: Technical Details
We implement a Hidden Markov Model following
the standard formulation of
Rabiner (
1989
)
,
using four observable features computed

FCR

window), its standard deviation, the week-over-week price
change, and the
aFRR
acceptance rate.
These features are standardized before training.
The
HMM
is trained via Baum-Welch on the full
2020–2022 in-sample period and applied in a walk-forward
fashion from 2023 onward, with the state sequence
re-estimated monthly.
The four latent states correspond to identifiable market
regimes: normal (
FCR
≈
\approx
16 EUR/
MW
/h),
crisis (
FCR
≥
\geq
20 EUR/
MW
/h), post-crisis
normalization (
FCR
declining rapidly), and
low-volatility (
FCR
stable at low levels).
Regime assignment is obtained from the most
likely latent state sequence under the fitted
model, using the Viterbi algorithm.
The Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) agent
(Haarnoja
et al.
,
2018
)
receives the current
HMM
state as input and outputs a bid percentile
p
∈
[
Q
20
,
Q
60
]
p\in[Q_{20},Q_{60}]
, trained to maximize realized
weekly
FCR
revenue.
Training uses 2020–2022 data with a replay buffer of
52 weeks and a discount factor
γ
=
0.95
\gamma=0.95
.
The policy is fixed from 2023 onward (no online learning
in the out-of-sample period).
Validation on a held-out 2022 H2 window confirmed that
the policy improves
FCR
acceptance rate relative to
the static
Q
40
Q_{40}
strategy in all four regime states,
with the largest gain in the post-crisis normalization
state (acceptance rate: 34%
→
\to
58%).
Out-of-sample performance is stable across all four
regimes, with no evidence of overfitting in the
held-out 2022 H2 validation window.
The
HMM
+SAC system adds €62,547 over three
out-of-sample years relative to the static
Q
40
Q_{40}
baseline (+3.4%), with the improvement concentrated in
the 2023 regime transition period.
The module is modular and can be replaced with any
regime-detection mechanism without affecting the Layer 1
LP structure or the Layer 3
DP
