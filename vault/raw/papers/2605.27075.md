Abstract
Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) achieve strong visual quality, but their iterative denoising process requires many costly Transformer evaluations. Training-free acceleration methods reduce this cost by caching, forecasting, or verifying intermediate features, yet the runtime decision of when to execute a Full step is often driven by fixed schedules or hand-tuned thresholds. We propose
SoftCap
, a training-free control layer for cache-based DiT inference. SoftCap couples a Trajectory Drift Observer, which estimates local cache risk from lightweight hidden-state statistics, with a Soft-Budget PI Controller, which adjusts the Full-triggering threshold from realized compute relative to a fixed reference profile. The budget is a soft ceiling: it shapes the threshold but does not require a run to spend a prescribed number of Full evaluations. On FLUX.1-dev, SoftCap improves over SpeCa at a comparable middle-compute operating point, raising ImageReward from 0.967 to 0.981 and reducing LPIPS-Full from 0.518 to 0.498 at nearly identical FLOPs, while target-sweep diagnostics show the intended soft-ceiling behavior as the budget is relaxed.

Introduction
Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) have become a central backbone for high-fidelity visual generation, but their inference cost remains high. A single sample requires many denoising evaluations, and each evaluation runs a large Transformer network over high-dimensional latent tokens. This cost is especially visible in recent text-to-image and video systems, where generation latency and serving throughput are constrained not only by the number of sampling steps but also by the per-step cost of attention and feed-forward blocks. Reducing this cost without retraining the base model is therefore a practical requirement for deploying large DiTs.
Training-free feature caching has emerged as a practical way to reduce the per-step cost of diffusion inference. Rather than executing all Transformer blocks at every denoising step, cache-based methods reuse or approximate intermediate features. Representative examples include DeepCache-style feature reuse and TaylorSeer-style trajectory forecasting
(Ma
et al.
,
2024
; Liu
et al.
,
2025c
)
; speculative feature caching further studies when a predicted feature should be accepted
(Liu
et al.
,
2025d
)
. Together, these methods show that diffusion trajectories contain substantial temporal redundancy and have advanced the design of cache engines and feature predictors.
The remaining question is when a runtime system should trust a cached approximation. Many Full/Cache policies still rely on static schedules, fixed thresholds, or manually selected acceptance rules. Such open-loop policies miss an important asymmetry: the value of a Full evaluation depends on both local approximation risk and the amount of computation already spent along the current sample trajectory. When the run is close to its compute ceiling, a moderate-risk cached approximation may be acceptable; when the run has spent less compute than expected, a high-risk step may justify a Full evaluation. Conversely, if the trajectory remains stable, executing additional Full steps only to meet a nominal target may bring little marginal benefit. DiT acceleration therefore needs a policy layer that regulates Full evaluations under a compute ceiling, in addition to a cache engine that approximates features.
We propose SoftCap, a training-free soft-budget control framework for accelerating DiTs. SoftCap treats Full/Cache selection as a feedback-controlled decision rather than a fixed caching schedule. It combines a Trajectory Drift Observer (TDO), which estimates the risk of continuing cached approximation from lightweight drift cues, with a Soft-Budget PI Controller, which adapts the Full-triggering threshold according to realized compute. A final Risk-Gated Full/Cache Decision compares the observer risk with the controller threshold and selects either a Full evaluation or the underlying cache engine. The design deliberately separates local trajectory risk from global compute state: the observer asks whether the current approximation looks risky, while the controller asks how strict the risk gate should be under the current soft budget.
A key property of SoftCap is that its budget parameter is a
soft computational ceiling
, not a required spending target. The controller can make Full evaluations easier or harder to trigger, but it does not force the system to spend computation when the observed trajectory risk remains low. This behavior is useful for deployment: a user-specified compute limit should shape latency without requiring unnecessary model evaluations.
We evaluate SoftCap on FLUX.1-dev with a 200-prompt text-to-image benchmark. Under a consistent generated-vs-full evaluation protocol, SoftCap provides a competitive quality-efficiency tradeoff against representative caching, forecasting, and speculative baselines. Target-sweep diagnostics further support the soft-ceiling interpretation: relaxing the budget raises the allowed compute, but the realized number of Full evaluations saturates when the observer does not identify enough high-risk steps. Detailed metrics and matched-compute comparisons are provided in the experimental section.

Related Work
2.1
Efficient Diffusion and DiT Inference
Diffusion models generate samples through iterative denoising, which makes inference more expensive than single-pass generative models. DiTs further increase the per-step cost because their denoising networks rely on Transformer blocks with attention and feed-forward computation over many latent tokens
(Peebles and Xie,
2023
)
. Existing acceleration methods reduce either the number of denoising evaluations or the cost of each evaluation. Step-reduction methods include improved numerical solvers, distillation, consistency-style modeling, and consistency transfer for multimodal diffusion systems
(Qiu
et al.
,
2025b
)
, but they may require a different sampler or additional training. SoftCap focuses on the complementary setting: the sampling schedule and base model are kept intact, and the per-step network cost is reduced by training-free feature caching.
2.2
Feature Caching and Forecasting
Feature caching exploits temporal redundancy across nearby denoising steps. Early cache-based methods reuse intermediate activations from previous steps to skip parts of the denoising network

et al.

Forecasting-based methods address this issue by predicting the current feature from historical states. TaylorSeer models feature trajectories with finite differences and Taylor expansion

et al.

; PrediT uses linear multistep forecasting with a corrector for high-dynamics regions

(Chen

(Qiu

.
These methods primarily improve the approximation mechanism: how cached features are reused, forecasted, decomposed, or corrected. SoftCap addresses a different layer of the acceleration pipeline. Given a cache engine that can produce an approximate hidden state, SoftCap controls when the approximation should be accepted under the current risk and compute state.
2.3
Adaptive Caching and Verification Policies
Several recent methods improve caching by making the decision more adaptive. ToCa observes that tokens have different sensitivity to cached approximation and applies token-wise feature caching
(Zou
et al.
,
2024
)
. TAP uses a low-cost probe to select token-level predictors
(Zhu
et al.
,
2026
)
. ClusCa clusters spatial tokens and computes representative tokens within each cluster
(Zheng
et al.
,
2025
)
. RFC estimates output-feature changes from input-output relations and uses relational cache scheduling to trigger recomputation when expected changes are large
(Son
et al.
,
2026
)
. Hybrid-grained caching further shows that cache granularity can differ across model components, such as block-level and prompt-level reuse in controllable generation
(Liu
et al.
,
2026
)
. These works show that uniform caching policies are often insufficient because approximation risk varies across tokens, layers, samples, denoising stages, and model components.
Speculative verification offers another perspective on the Full/Cache decision. SpeCa, for example, predicts intermediate features with a lightweight draft mechanism and verifies whether the drafted features should be accepted
(Liu
et al.
,
2025d
)
. This line of work studies the reliability of a proposed approximation. SoftCap uses a different abstraction: it does not introduce a draft-and-verify pipeline, but places a feedback policy around a cache engine. Its observer summarizes runtime trajectory state through drift cues, and its controller adjusts the Full-triggering threshold according to realized compute. This shifts the policy question from only вҖңis this approximation reliable?вҖқ to вҖңhow strict should the risk gate be under a soft compute ceiling?вҖқ
2.4
Budget-Aware Compute Allocation
The most relevant distinction of SoftCap is its treatment of compute allocation. Existing caching methods often report results at different acceleration ratios, but the runtime policy is rarely formulated as a closed-loop budget controller. SoftCap maintains a soft budget ceiling, a fixed reference progress profile, and the realized number of Full evaluations. The discrepancy between the reference and realized compute controls the risk threshold, while the Trajectory Drift Observer supplies the local approximation-risk signal. This decoupling makes the role of each component explicit: the observer estimates trajectory-dependent risk, the controller regulates how strict the decision should be under the current compute state, and the final gate converts risk crossings into Full evaluations. SoftCap therefore acts as a control layer for cache-based DiT acceleration rather than a replacement for existing cache engines or feature predictors.

3.1
Preliminaries
Diffusion Transformer inference.
A diffusion model generates a sample by iteratively denoising a noisy latent. At each denoising step, a neural denoiser predicts an update direction and the sampler advances the latent to the next noise level. In DiTs, the denoiser is implemented as a stack of Transformer blocks over latent tokens. Let

Feature caching.
Training-free caching reduces this cost by reusing or approximating hidden features at selected steps. A cache engine stores an anchor feature from a recent Full evaluation and uses it to approximate later features. If the approximation is accepted, expensive downstream block computation can be skipped; if it is rejected, the model performs a Full evaluation and refreshes the cache state. SoftCap operates in this cached-inference setting. It is cache-engine agnostic in principle, and we instantiate it with a Taylor-based cache engine in our experiments.

5
Conclusion
In this paper, We presented SoftCap, a training-free soft-budget control framework for cache-based DiT acceleration. Instead of treating Full/Cache scheduling as a fixed schedule or a manually selected threshold, SoftCap separates two decisions that are often conflated: whether the current cached trajectory appears risky, and how strict the Full-triggering gate should be under the realized compute state. The Trajectory Drift Observer estimates local cache risk from lightweight drift statistics, while the Soft-Budget PI Controller adjusts the dynamic threshold using a fixed reference budget profile and the realized number of Full evaluations.
Experiments on FLUX.1-dev show that SoftCap provides competitive quality-efficiency tradeoffs under the same evaluation protocol, with its clearest matched-compute gain in the middle FLOPs regime. Mechanism diagnostics further support the soft-ceiling interpretation: increasing the budget ceiling changes the decision threshold, but it does not require the run to execute additional Full evaluations when the observer does not find enough high-risk steps. This makes SoftCap a policy layer for regulating cache-based acceleration, rather than a replacement for cache engines themselves. Future work can extend this control view to additional cache mechanisms, denoising horizons, and multimodal generation backbones.
Limitations
Although SoftCap demonstrates promising performance, there can sometimes be a substantial gap between the actual number of Full steps executed and the predefined target budget. In future work, we will explore adaptive reference accumulation curves to achieve more precise alignment with the computational budget, thereby maximizing generation quality under a given computational quota.
