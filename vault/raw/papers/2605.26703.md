Abstract
The classic concept of “calibrated
forecasts” and its more recent refinement,
“calibeating,” are defined with respect
to the standard quadratic scoring rule. We extend these notions to the class
of
proper
scoring rules (for which the best forecast is the true
distribution) and define
proper-calibration
and
proper-calibeating
by requiring the errors to converge to zero uniformly
over all bounded proper scoring rules. We first establish that calibration
always implies proper-calibration, whereas calibeating need not imply
proper-calibeating. Second, we show how to guarantee proper-calibeating and
proper-multicalibeating. Finally, we demonstrate the equivalence between
proper-calibration and universal no regret when best replying to forecasts
in decision-making under uncertainty.

Introduction
Forecasting the probability of future events is a foundational problem.
Forecasters issue probabilistic predictions that are then compared with the
outcomes that materialize. How should one evaluate such a forecaster? A
natural approach is to use a
scoring rule
: a penalty function that
assigns a loss to each forecast–outcome pair. A scoring rule is deemed
proper
if the loss is minimized when the forecast equals the
underlying probability distribution of the outcome. The standard and most
widely used proper scoring rule is the
quadratic
rule (Brier 1950;
see Savage 1971, Schervish 1989, and the comprehensive treatment in Gneiting
and Raftery 2007).
A classic result (Sanders 1963; Murphy 1972; DeGroot and Fienberg 1983) is
that the Brier quadratic score

the forecasts are to the realized frequencies—and

is the
refinement
score—measuring how informative the partitioning of the
outcomes into bins is, where the partitioning is determined by the announced
forecasts (see Section
2.2
for the precise setup and
definitions). A surprising result of Foster and Vohra (1998) is that one can
generate forecasts that are
guaranteed
to be calibrated (i.e.,
𝒦

converges to zero), no matter what the outcomes turn out to be
(for the ensuing literature, see the survey of Olszewski 2015

In Foster and Hart (2023), we introduced the notion of “calibeating”: beating a reference forecaster by achieving
a Brier score no worse than its refinement score—thus gaining calibration
without losing expertise, which, as we argued there, is reflected in the
induced partitioning into bins and the resulting refinement score. We showed
that calibeating can be achieved in several ways: by a simple deterministic
online procedure; by a stochastic procedure that is itself calibrated; and
by a deterministic continuously calibrated procedure. All these results,
however, were stated exclusively for the quadratic score.

In Appendix A.9 of the full version of the paper, Foster and Hart (2022), we

However, there is an extensive class of proper scoring rules (in particular,
every decision-making setting under uncertainty generates one; see below and

guarantees are robust or merely artifacts of the quadratic functional form.
If a forecaster’s performance evaluation is sensitive to the specific choice
of a proper loss function, its theoretical and practical relevance may well
be questionable. This raises the natural question:
If a procedure is calibrated (or calibeats) under the quadratic score,
do these guarantees persist under every other proper scoring rule?
This paper answers that question for proper scoring rules that are bounded.

Achieving proper-calibeating.
We next provide three positive
results on proper-calibeating (i.e., calibeating simultaneously under all
bounded proper scoring rules).
First,
we show that a stochastic procedure that calibeats a finer
binning—one that cross-classifies forecasts by the bins of both the
reference forecaster and the procedure itself—is proper-calibeating as
well as proper-calibrated. A key insight is that it is not enough to
calibeat the reference forecaster and to be calibrated separately; what
matters is calibeating the joint binning, which keeps track of how the two
sets of bins interact (Appendix
A.2
elaborates further on this).
Second,
the very simple deterministic procedure of Theorem 3 of
Foster and Hart (2023)—which forecasts the running average action in the
reference forecaster’s bin—is shown to be proper-calibeating for every
Lipschitz proper scoring rule (which we term “proper-Li-calibeating”). We further show that this result
does not hold for all bounded proper scoring rules.
Third,
we establish the existence of a deterministic procedure that
is simultaneously proper-Li-calibeating and continuously proper-calibrated;
this is obtained via an approximate decomposition of the score (that
generalizes from the quadratic case; see Appendix A.7 in Foster and Hart
2026).
All three results extend to the simultaneous proper-calibeating of multiple
reference forecasters, by cross-classifying across all of their bins.
Decision-making under uncertainty.
Forecasts are commonly used in
decision-making under uncertainty, where the probabilities of the various
states of nature are unknown (see Section
6
). The forecast is
utilized to make a decision that optimizes the decision-maker’s expected
utility. This process induces a scoring rule, where the loss is taken as the
realized disutility of the decision that is optimal with respect to the
forecast. The scoring rule is proper: when the forecast is “correct,” i.e., equal to the true distribution, expected
utility is maximized and expected loss is minimized. We show that, in a
sequential decision-making setting, the calibration score of the sequence of
forecasts, computed according to the induced scoring rule, is exactly the
“regret” incurred by best-replying to
these forecasts. Consequently, proper-calibration is equivalent to achieving
“universal” no-regret, simultaneously
across all bounded utility functions. Similarly, proper-calibeating yields
appropriate gain guarantees across all utilities.

Related Work
The question of how forecasting guarantees extend across different scoring
rules has attracted attention from several directions.
Closest to our work, Chen, Huang, Jordan, and Luo (2026) prove calibeating
rates for general proper losses with a reduction to regret minimization.
Lee, Noarov, Pai, and Roth (2022) study multicalibeating through online
multiobjective optimization. Universality across losses is also central to
the “U-calibration” framework of
Kleinberg, Leme, Schneider, and Teng (2023) and the “omniprediction” framework of Gopalan, Kalai, Reingold,
Sharan, and Wieder (2022). On the statistical side, Dimitriadis, Gneiting,
and Jordan (2021) and Popordanoska, Gruber, Tiulpin, Buettner, and Blaschko
(2023) analyze the calibration–refinement decomposition for general proper
rules. Additional connections between proper losses and calibration appear
in Blasiok, Gopalan, Hu, and Nakkiran (2023) and Luo, Senapati, and Sharan
(2025).
