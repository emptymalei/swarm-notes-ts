title: Systematic investigation of self-report informativeness
---

**Background:** Explanations are often evaluated using self-reported subjective measures alongside objective measures like forward simulation accuracy, but the agreement between these two types of measures is often inconsistent.

**Question / Future Work:** The conditions under which self-reported subjective explanation ratings reliably track demonstrated human understanding (like forward simulation accuracy) need systematic investigation, as current findings suggest this correspondence only occurs under favorable conditions, namely when explanations are fully correct and the participant has successfully learned the decision pattern.

**Why It Matters:** Establishing when subjective user feedback is a reliable proxy for objective understanding is vital for designing efficient human-centered evaluations, as current methods risk over-relying on uninformative subjective data.

**Evidence:** A related question is whether explanation correctness moderates the link between self-reported and demonstrated understanding, as self-reports tracked performance only when explanations were fully correct. If this pattern replicates beyond our specific task, the disconnect may be partly context-dependent rather than a fixed limitation of subjective measures, and the conditions under which self-reports are informative deserve systematic investigation.
