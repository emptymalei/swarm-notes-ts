title: Mechanistic Identifiability of Ecological HOIs
---

**Background:** Higher-order ecological interactions are often inferred from time-series data using phenomenological models, but such models frequently suffer from structural non-identifiability. In many cases, higher-order mechanisms are effectively captured by simpler pairwise interaction models, making the true interaction structure difficult to distinguish from time-series data alone.

**Question / Future Work:** There is a critical need to develop frameworks that can reliably disentangle the true underlying mechanistic structure of ecological systems from observed time-series data. Future research should prioritize identifying how to integrate independent ecological information—such as life-history traits, phenological data, and experimentally validated, biologically-justified interaction constraints—to break the degeneracy between higher-order and effective pairwise representations.

**Why It Matters:** This is a foundational problem because currently, model selection based solely on goodness-of-fit to time-series data is insufficient to claim the existence of higher-order interactions, which may be critical for predicting system responses to environmental stressors or interventions.

**Evidence:** Consequently, higher-order interactions cannot, in general, be inferred from time-series data alone. We further identify a fundamental problem of mechanistic identifiability, whereby distinct interaction mechanisms generate nearly indistinguishable dynamics, potentially leading to accurate yet misleading ecological interpretations.
