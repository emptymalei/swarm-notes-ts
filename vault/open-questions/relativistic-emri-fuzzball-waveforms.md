title: Relativistic EMRI Fuzzball Modeling
---

**Background:** The fuzzball paradigm proposes that black holes are horizonless, quantum-gravitational objects whose structure at the would-be event horizon scale deviates from the classical Kerr metric. Testing these predictions requires modeling extreme-mass-ratio inspirals (EMRIs) around non-Kerr geometries and assessing the sensitivity of space-based gravitational-wave detectors.

**Question / Future Work:** Current models for testing the fuzzball paradigm with EMRIs rely on semi-analytic frameworks based on simplified Newtonian or post-Newtonian orbital dynamics, which may not capture all necessary strong-field relativistic effects. There is a need to develop more rigorous waveform models that incorporate relativistic orbital motion as geodesics within specific, analytically defined spacetimes of exotic compact objects, such as fuzzball or topological star configurations. Currently, the lack of such exact analytic metric representations for generic quantum-gravity-motivated compact objects hinders the development of fully relativistic EMRI templates.

**Why It Matters:** The ability to distinguish between a classical Kerr black hole and a fuzzball depends heavily on the fidelity of the waveform model in the strong-field regime near the horizon, necessitating a shift beyond current phenomenological/Newtonian approximations toward relativistic geodesic-based models.

**Evidence:** A complementary research direction is to incorporate relativistic orbital effects in the EMRI dynamics, by modeling the motion as geodesics of a perturbed stationary and axisymmetric spacetime describing the fuzzball model. Although this would provide a more faithful description of the strong-field regime, it is very challenging due to the limited knowledge of the analytic form of the metric of these exotic compact objects.
