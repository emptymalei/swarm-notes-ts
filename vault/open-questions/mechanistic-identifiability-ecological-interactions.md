title: Mechanistic Identifiability of Ecological Interactions
---

**Background:** Ecological models often struggle to distinguish between systems governed by intrinsic higher-order interactions and those whose dynamics can be sufficiently approximated by effective pairwise models. Even when high-resolution time-series data are available, the presence of higher-order interactions may be structurally non-identifiable, leading to valid predictive models that yield misleading ecological interpretations.

**Question / Future Work:** There is a need to develop integrated methodological frameworks that go beyond pure time-series inference to identify higher-order interactions. Future work should focus on methods that combine abundance trajectories with supplementary data—such as life-history information, phenological data, or experimentally derived constraints on non-additive interaction mechanisms—to break the structural indistinguishability between higher-order and effective pairwise models. Furthermore, research is required to determine the conditions under which higher-order interaction structures can be rigorously reduced to effective pairwise descriptions and to clarify the resulting ecological implications for coexistence and stability.

**Why It Matters:** This is a central bottleneck in ecological modeling because it addresses the core issue of mechanistic identifiability. Without resolving this, researchers risk applying models that are predictively convenient but ecologically and mechanistically incorrect, particularly when predicting outcomes of perturbations like management actions or environmental changes.

**Evidence:** Our results highlight the need to complement time-series data with additional ecological information to infer interaction structure reliably.
