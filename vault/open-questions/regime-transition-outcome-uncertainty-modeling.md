title: Regime transition outcome uncertainty
---

**Background:** Caldera unrest is characterized by nonlinear and coupled interactions where surface observables like deformation and seismicity accelerate as the crust approaches a critical mechanical threshold. Distinguishing between simple exponential growth and finite-time singularity models is central to forecasting the timing and nature of regime transitions in these systems.

**Question / Future Work:** While finite-time singularity models can successfully quantify accelerating dynamics and estimate a critical time horizon (t_c) for a potential regime shift, the physical nature of the outcome—such as an eruption versus a bradyseismic peak—remains non-deterministic. Future work must develop coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical-chemical models to map these critical times to specific physical outcomes.

**Why It Matters:** This is a fundamental limitation in volcanic hazard assessment, as identifying that a system is approaching a critical state is insufficient for planning if the specific physical consequences cannot be reliably predicted.

**Evidence:** Although no evidence of imminent eruption is found, the system appears to be approaching a critical mechanical threshold whose outcome remains uncertain.
