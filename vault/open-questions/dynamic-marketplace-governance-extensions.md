title: Dynamic marketplace governance extensions
---

**Background:** Marketplaces commonly route order flow among third-party sellers, who generally manage their inventories using standard replenishment policies based on observed demand history. Platform allocation rules determine the information content of these demand streams, fundamentally influencing sellers' forecasting accuracy and inventory safety-stock levels.

**Question / Future Work:** While the authors establish an optimal framework for a stationary environment with optimal forecasters, future research is needed to determine the platform's optimal allocation strategies under non-stationary demand (e.g., seasonality, trends, and regime shifts) and how such dynamic allocations impact marketplace stability and operational bottlenecks. Additionally, extending this to a fully endogenous model that accounts for pricing, seller learning, and platform competition is essential for a broader theory of marketplace governance.

**Why It Matters:** Extending this framework to non-stationary and strategic environments is necessary to translate these theoretical insights into practical, robust operational strategies for real-world digital marketplaces.

**Evidence:** Extending the analysis to nonstationary demand (seasonality, trends, regime shifts) and time-varying allocations would connect the mechanism to realistic planning environments... Finally, integrating strategic behavior, including pricing, learning, and platform competition, would broaden the framework from inventory shaping to a more comprehensive theory of marketplace operations under governance constraints.
