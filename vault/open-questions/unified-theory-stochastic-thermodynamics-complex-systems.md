title: Unified Theory for Complex Systems
---

**Background:** Stochastic thermodynamics has historically relied on the assumption of local detailed balance to link statistical irreversibility to physical energy dissipation at the microscale. Extending this framework to macroscopic, complex, or non-physical systems poses challenges as this direct link often breaks down.

**Question / Future Work:** It remains unclear whether a unified thermodynamic framework exists that can consistently quantify irreversibility and energy costs across disparate scales—from microscopic molecular machines to macroscopic biological, social, and computational systems—where the traditional connection between statistical irreversibility and physical energy dissipation is no longer direct or well-defined. Assessing the potential for such a unified theory, and determining the robustness and physical meaningfulness of stochastic thermodynamic measures in non-physical contexts, constitutes a significant open challenge.

**Why It Matters:** This question addresses the fundamental scope and ultimate validity of applying stochastic thermodynamics outside its original domain, which is the central theme of the perspective. Tracking this will show whether the field matures into a universal theory of complex systems or remains a collection of disjoint, context-specific methods.

**Evidence:** Whether a unified formulation encompassing these diverse extensions exists remains an open question. In particular, it remains to be seen to what extent methods from stochastic thermodynamics can provide robust and quantitatively meaningful insights in macroscopic or non-physical contexts, where the connection to energy dissipation is no longer direct.
