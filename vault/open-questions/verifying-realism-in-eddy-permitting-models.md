title: Realism of eddy-permitting models
---

**Background:** Eddy-permitting ocean models, which are defined by a grid resolution of approximately 1/4°, are widely used to study mesoscale ocean eddies despite persistent uncertainties regarding their ability to realistically capture eddy dynamics. Validating these models against observational data remains a critical requirement for justifying their use in downstream scientific applications, such as biogeochemical modeling and climate forecasting.

**Question / Future Work:** The extent to which 1/4° eddy-permitting models accurately represent mesoscale eddy dynamics remains an unresolved problem, as these models consistently exhibit significant biases in eddy amplitude, lifetime, radius, and propagation characteristics compared to satellite altimetry observations. The research explicitly identifies the lack of verified model realism as a fundamental bottleneck for any downstream scientific testing of hypotheses involving biogeochemistry, ocean physics, or other Earth system components that rely on the assumption of well-resolved, realistic eddies.

**Why It Matters:** This is a foundational validation challenge for the field of climate modeling; if eddy-permitting models fail to produce realistic eddy demographics, then all subsequent research outputs based on these models (e.g., nutrient transport, heat redistribution, ecosystem structures) are potentially flawed.

**Evidence:** Despite their critical role in understanding and forecasting climate characteristics, these so-called eddy-permitting models have not been explored to verify that resolved eddies are realistic, and thus any downstream scientific testing of hypotheses in biogeochemistry, ocean physics or other associated Earth systems impacted by eddies hinge on this critical assumption.
