title: Constrained vs. Unconstrained CMB Anomaly Testing
---

**Background:** Large-scale statistical anomalies in CMB temperature maps, such as dipole modulation, lack of large-angle correlations, quadrupole-octopole alignment, and point-parity asymmetry, have been observed across multiple experiments, but remain inconclusive as either statistical fluctuations or evidence of new physics. Polarization data provides an independent, necessary probe to distinguish these possibilities.

**Question / Future Work:** Future investigations must rigorously test whether the observed CMB temperature anomalies are reflected in the corresponding E-mode polarization field. This involves moving beyond the current reliance on unconstrained Lambda-CDM simulations—which assume the polarization field is a statistically independent Gaussian realization—to constrained realizations that account for the observed temperature anisotropies. A robust detection or consistent null result using these constrained methods is required to definitively evaluate the 'fluke' hypothesis.

**Why It Matters:** Determining whether E-mode polarization inherits the temperature anomalies is critical for rejecting or supporting the 'fluke' hypothesis, as unconstrained simulations may not accurately capture the statistical expectations if the underlying physics is connected across temperature and polarization channels.

**Evidence:** Testing the fluke hypothesis therefore involves a direct comparison between the anomaly statistics obtained from actual polarization data and the distribution derived from constrained simulations, rather than from their unconstrained counterparts.
