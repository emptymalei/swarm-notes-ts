title: Exoplanetary Polarimetry Sensitivity Limits
---

**Background:** Linear polarization signals in exoplanet transits are predicted to be significantly lower than the current instrumental noise floors, typically on the order of 10^-4% to 10^-1% depending on the host star and planet. Detecting these requires extremely high polarimetric precision and stability in instrumentation.

**Question / Future Work:** Current high-cadence polarimetric observations of exoplanet transits remain limited by instrumental noise floors, preventing the detection of predicted subtle atmospheric polarization signatures. Systematic improvements in instrumental stability, waveplate calibration, and atmospheric noise mitigation are required to push polarimetric precision beyond current ~0.02% thresholds.

**Why It Matters:** Advancing polarimetric precision is a critical bottleneck for characterization of exoplanetary atmospheres, representing a frontier in observational astronomy.

**Evidence:** In the best cases, we achieved a polarization dispersion of σp ∼ 0.02% in the r band, which is considered excellent, yet still insufficient to detect the expected signal for these targets.
